I Built This Blower Because My Father-in-Law Couldn't Do His Own Yard Work Anymore

A personal note from Daniel Seese — inventor of the Seese Pro™ Ultra-Light Blower

Dear Friend,

Dear friend,

If you've ever set down a tool that used to be easy — and felt something heavier than just tiredness — this letter is for you.

My name is Daniel Seese. I'm an engineer, not a marketer. And the reason I built the Seese Pro™ is simple: I watched someone I love quietly lose something he'd worked his whole life to keep. His independence. And I couldn't just do nothing.

The Saturday Morning That Changed Everything

His name is Frank. My father-in-law. Sixty-eight years old, still sharp, still the man who shovels his own driveway before sunrise and fixes his own gutters without asking for help. That's his identity. Always has been.

So when my wife mentioned — quietly, over dinner one night — that Frank hadn't done his own yard work in nearly two years, I drove over the following Saturday to see for myself.

He was sitting in a lawn chair at the edge of his property. His neighbor's teenage son was raking his leaves.

I sat down next to him. He didn't say anything for a moment. Then: "It's the blower. Thing weighs more than my granddaughter. I can't hold it for twenty minutes anymore." A pause. "I just… can't."

He set his coffee mug down on the armrest.

I drove home and went straight into my garage.

That was two years ago. Frank now does his own yard work every Saturday, alone, in under thirty minutes. And that's when everything changed — not just for Frank, but for thousands of people I'd never met. A few months after I started shipping the Seese Pro™, I got a note from Ted, 67, in Portland, Oregon, who'd used it on his driveway one Sunday morning.

"I cleaned my entire 40-foot driveway — every corner, every crack — in four and a half minutes. Didn't set it down once. My old gas blower used to take me twenty-five minutes and leave me in bed for the rest of the morning. I actually laughed out loud when I finished."
— Ted, 67, Portland, OR

What I Went Into My Garage to Build

The problem with most blowers isn't the power. It's the weight. A standard gas blower asks you to hold four, five, sometimes six kilograms for twenty minutes straight. At thirty, that's tiring. At sixty-three, it's a punishment your back doesn't forget.

So I started with one question: what's the lightest possible tool that still moves serious air?

The answer was brushless motor technology — the same type used in high-end drones and electric vehicles. No fuel to mix. No pull cord. No warm-up. Just one button, and 200 miles per hour of wind in one hand. I packed it into a frame that weighs nine hundred and fifty grams. Less than two pounds. About the same as a bottle of wine.

The battery lasts forty minutes per charge — more than enough to clean a large patio, dry a full-size car, and clear a frost-covered windshield in one pass. And here's the part that still surprises people: a 35-square-meter patio takes less than five minutes. Not twenty-five. Five. That's not a marketing number — that's what happens when 200 MPH meets a leaf pile.

I gave the first prototype to Frank. He used it the following Saturday. He didn't say much — Frank rarely does. But my wife called that afternoon: "Dad did the whole yard himself. He looks like himself again."

That call made everything worth it. And then, a few weeks later, I heard from Margaret, in Columbus, Ohio.

"I dried my car in three minutes — without a single cloth, without a single scratch — for the first time in years. I used to dread wash day. Now I actually look forward to it. I feel like I've got my Saturday mornings back."
— Margaret, 71, Columbus, OH
