What Our Investigation Found

We spent 30 days investigating the growing trend of seniors abandoning traditional outdoor tools. What we discovered was startling.

  • Finding #1: The average gas-powered leaf blower weighs between 8 and 12 lbs — and that's before you add fuel. For adults over 60, repetitive overhead and lateral motion with that kind of weight is a leading cause of rotator cuff injuries and acute back strain.
  • Finding #2: According to the Consumer Product Safety Commission, nearly 400,000 Americans are treated in emergency rooms each year for injuries related to lawn and garden equipment. A disproportionate number are over the age of 55.
  • Finding #3: A new category of ultra-light cordless blowers — weighing under 2.2 lbs — has been quietly outselling gas models in dozens of online marketplaces. The leader? A device called the Seese Pro™, which weighs just 950 grams and delivers 200 MPH of airspeed.
  • Finding #4: Most homeowners who own a gas blower admit they use it less than 6 times per year — not because they don't need it, but because getting it out, fueling it up, and dealing with the physical toll isn't worth it for a quick 15-minute job.
  • Finding #5: The same homeowners report spending an average of 25 minutes sweeping or raking their patios by hand — a task that can be done in under 5 minutes with the right tool.

The pattern was impossible to ignore. The tools most Americans have in their garages were designed for a different era — and a different body.

A Physical Therapist Speaks Out: "I Tell My Patients to Stop Using Heavy Tools"

We reached out to a physical therapist who works primarily with adults over 55. What she told us should be required reading for anyone still wrestling with a 10-lb gas blower.

"The number one thing I see in my clinic every fall and spring? Back and shoulder injuries from yard work. And it's almost never from one dramatic incident. It's cumulative. People think they're fine because the pain doesn't hit until the next morning — or the next week. But every time you hold a heavy, vibrating tool at arm's length, you're placing enormous stress on your lumbar spine and your rotator cuff. For adults over 60, whose connective tissue is already less elastic, this is genuinely dangerous.

What I tell my patients now is simple: the tool should do the work, not your body. I'd much rather see someone using a light, well-engineered cordless device than white-knuckling a gas blower for an hour. The difference in physical demand is not marginal — it's night and day."

She's not alone in this view. An increasing number of occupational therapists and geriatric specialists are now factoring tool weight and ergonomics into their recommendations for active older adults.
