A Letter From the Man Who Refused to Call His Kids for Help

How a retired contractor built a 2-pound blower that outperforms $300 gas models — and why he's offering it at cost this week only

LIFESTYLE

A silver-haired man in his mid-60s sitting on a step inside a garage, looking down at a heavy gas blower resting on the floor beside him. Morning light comes through the open garage door. The mood is quiet and reflective — not defeated, just thinking.

Dear friend,

I need to tell you something I've never shared publicly before. Four years ago, on a Saturday morning in October, I walked into my garage, wrapped both hands around my old Husqvarna gas blower — the same one I'd used for fifteen years — and put it back down after thirty seconds. My lower back seized up so hard I had to sit on a bucket for ten minutes before I could walk inside.

I was 64 years old. I'd built houses with my own two hands for over three decades. And I couldn't blow leaves off my own driveway.

I sat in that garage for a long time. Not because of the pain — I'd dealt with worse on job sites. I sat there because of what it meant. And if you're reading this letter, I suspect you already know exactly what I mean.

My name is Tom Haskell. And I almost gave up on doing things myself.

I grew up watching my father maintain every inch of our property with his own hands. Not because he had to — because it was who he was. That pride passed down to me, and for forty years, I never thought twice about it. I mowed, I raked, I blew, I shoveled. My garage was organized like a hardware store. I was the guy the neighbors called when they needed advice on anything.

Then somewhere around my early sixties, things started to shift in ways I didn't expect. The gas blower that never bothered me before suddenly felt like lifting a cement block. After twenty minutes, my shoulders would ache. After thirty, I'd need a full day to recover. My wife started suggesting we hire someone to handle the yard. I smiled and said maybe. And I quietly went to bed that night feeling like something important was slipping away from me — something I hadn't given a name to yet, but could feel clearly.

I want to be honest with you, because I think you deserve honesty: I started avoiding certain jobs. Not because I didn't know how to do them — because the thought of the pain the next morning stopped me before I even started. Leaves would pile up on the back patio for a week before I worked up the motivation to deal with them. My driveway looked like I'd given up on it. And every time I walked past it, I felt something I hadn't expected to feel at my age: a quiet, stubborn shame.

That October morning in the garage was my breaking point. I wasn't going to accept this. I wasn't ready to hand my house — my home, the thing I'd spent thirty years maintaining with my own hands — over to someone else. So I did what I'd always done when something didn't work right: I started building a better way.

What I built — and why it surprised even me

I spent the better part of two years talking to engineers, ordering parts, and destroying prototypes in my own backyard. I wanted one thing that no blower on the market seemed willing to give me: enough power to actually move wet leaves and compacted debris, in a tool light enough to hold with one hand without my back filing a complaint the next morning. Every blower I tested made me choose between the two. Heavy and powerful, or light and useless. That compromise had always bothered me, and now I refused to live with it.

What we eventually built — what I now call the Seese Pro — weighs 950 grams. That's just over two pounds. Less than a bag of sugar. You hold it the same way you'd hold a cordless drill, one hand, arm relaxed at your side. But here's the part that still surprises me: it pushes air at over 200 miles per hour. That's more airspeed than most gas blowers I used on construction sites in my thirties. The brushless motor runs at 30,000 RPM — the same motor technology used in performance drones and electric vehicles. No gas mixing. No extension cord to trip over. No choke, no pull-cord, no ritual. You pick it up, press the trigger, and it works.

The first time I cleared my back patio — 400 square meters of flagstone I used to spend a morning on — I timed myself. Four minutes and forty seconds. My back felt nothing. I wasn't even breathing hard. I stood there for a moment, just looking at the clean stone, and I laughed out loud. My wife came outside to see what was funny. I told her I'd just gotten my Saturday mornings back. Then I walked over to the car, still wet from a wash I'd done the day before, and dried it completely in just over three minutes — every door gap, every mirror edge, every wheel arch — without a single towel touching the paint.
