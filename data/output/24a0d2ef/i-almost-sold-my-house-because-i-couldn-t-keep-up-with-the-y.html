I Almost Sold My House Because I Couldn't Keep Up With the Yard Anymore

A letter from the man who built a 2-pound blower that replaced everything in my garage — and gave me back the one thing I refused to lose.

HERO PHOTO
A man in his early 60s sitting quietly on a back porch, looking out at a leaf-covered yard. A large gas blower sits on the ground nearby, unused. His expression is tired and reflective — not dramatic, just honest. Warm autumn light, late afternoon. The mood is human and real.

Dear Friend,

Dear friend,

I'm going to tell you something I've never written publicly before. Three years ago, at 61 years old, I sat on my back porch after a particularly brutal Saturday of yard work, and I seriously considered selling my home of 22 years and moving into a condo. Not because I wanted to. Because I didn't think I could keep up with it anymore.

My name is David Harrington. I'm not a famous inventor. I'm not a Silicon Valley entrepreneur. I'm a mechanical engineer from outside Columbus, Ohio, who spent 30 years designing tools for industrial use — and never once imagined he'd end up building something for his own backyard. But that afternoon on the porch changed everything.

It had started slowly, the way most things do. My old gas blower — a heavy machine I'd owned since 2004 — had started to feel different. Not the machine. Me. The eight kilograms of dead weight that used to feel manageable now felt like carrying a small child around for 45 minutes straight. My right shoulder would ache by Tuesday. My lower back would remind me every morning for three days that I was no longer 40 years old, no matter how much I wanted to believe otherwise.

I tried to ignore it. I bought a back brace. I started breaking yard work into two sessions instead of one. I told my wife Carol it was "just smart planning." She didn't say anything. She didn't have to. I could feel what I was actually doing — I was shrinking. Task by task, hour by hour, I was quietly surrendering the parts of my home life I had always handled myself. And I resented every inch of it.

The leaf blower was the worst part. It wasn't just the weight. It was the whole ritual — prime the bulb, set the choke, yank the cord three times, four times, sometimes six, then adjust the choke again and rev it until it ran smooth. By the time I got the machine running, I was already tired before I'd blown a single leaf. The exhaust smell. The vibration up my arm. The noise — Lord, the noise. I wore earplugs just to use it in my own yard. My neighbor Gary, who is 73 and has a bad hip, had given up his gas blower entirely two years earlier and hired a lawn crew. I told him I'd never do that. He smiled at me the way you smile at someone who still believes they're right. I think about that smile a lot now.

That Saturday on the porch, I made a decision. Not the decision to sell the house — the other one. I decided I was going to fix this problem myself. Because that's the only thing I knew how to do.

After that afternoon, I went into engineer mode. Not to start a company — I genuinely just didn't want to feel that way anymore. I bought three different cordless blowers from the hardware store and pulled them apart on my workbench. None of them were good enough. Too weak, too short on battery life, or still too bulky and two-handed to use comfortably for more than ten minutes. So I started reaching out to motor manufacturers I'd worked with over the years — specifically the people building brushless motors for industrial drones and electric vehicle systems — because I knew from 30 years of engineering that these motors could produce serious airflow without adding serious weight. That was the piece everyone else had missed.

What I ended up building, after fourteen months of prototyping and about as many arguments with Carol over the workshop taking over the garage, is what I now call the Seese Pro. It weighs 950 grams. About the same as two cups of coffee. You hold it in one hand — not two hands gripped and braced against vibration, one hand, relaxed, the way you'd hold a flashlight on an evening walk. And it moves air at 200 miles per hour. The same airspeed as a professional-grade blower that weighs four times as much and costs twice the price. The motor runs at 30,000 RPM using the same brushless technology inside Tesla motors. I'm an engineer — I don't say that to impress you, I say it because I want you to understand that the power in this machine is real. It's just been put in a body that doesn't punish you for using it.

The first time I used the prototype in my backyard, I cleaned my entire 400-square-foot patio in under five minutes. I stood there afterward — not tired, not sore, not planning my recovery schedule for the next three days. Just standing. Carol came outside and asked if something was wrong because I looked strange just standing there doing nothing. I told her I was better than okay. I hadn't said that about yard work in years. I've said it a lot since.

I started using it for everything. Drying my truck after a wash — three minutes, no water spots, no chamois cramping my hand. Clearing the garage, the gutters, the workshop, the car vents. That winter, I used it to clear the snow off my windshield before my son-in-law had even found his ice scraper. He asked me where he could get one. That was the moment I realized I probably wasn't the only person who needed this.

I started hearing from customers about six months after we launched. The messages I received were not what I expected. I thought people would write to say the blower was powerful or convenient or good value. They said those things too. But that's not what stopped me in my tracks.

I got a letter from a woman named Margaret, 71 years old, from Columbus. Her daughter had given her a Seese Pro as a birthday gift. She wrote to me by hand — an actual letter, mailed in an envelope — and this is what she said:

"David, I want you to know that I cried the first time I used this. Not from frustration, like I used to. From relief. I cleaned my entire driveway in six minutes and I wasn't even breathing hard. I feel like myself again. I didn't realize how much I had missed that feeling."
— Margaret, 71, Columbus OH

I printed that letter and pinned it to my workbench. It's still there today.

Last month, a retired contractor from Tennessee named Bill — 67 years old, two rotator cuff surgeries behind him — sent me a photo of himself holding the Seese Pro above his head with one arm, grinning like a kid on Christmas morning. He wrote: "First blower I've used in three years that doesn't remind me I had surgery." I sent him a handwritten thank-you note. I meant every word of it.

So why am I writing to you today, specifically? Here is the honest truth, and I'm going to be direct because I think you deserve that: we are sitting on a final production run of the Seese Pro, and I've made the decision to offer it at a price that is below where it would normally be. $119.99, reduced from $249.99 — that's 55% off. I want to explain why I'm doing this, because it's not a gimmick. I'm 64 years old. I didn't build this company to become wealthy. I built it because I was frustrated and I had the skills to solve a real problem. But running a consumer product business is harder than a mechanical engineer expects, and I'm at a point in my life where I need to focus on what matters most. What matters most to me is getting this tool into the hands of people who will actually use it — people like me, like Margaret, like Bill — before the remaining stock is gone. I'm being honest about that because I respect your time and I respect the decision you're about to make.

Every Seese Pro I ship comes with a lifetime replacement guarantee. Not a limited warranty with fine print on page three. A replacement. If it fails, I replace it. No forms, no phone trees, no runaround. That guarantee exists because I built this machine and I know what it's built to do. I'm standing behind it with my name on it. And there's a full 30-day money-back guarantee on top of that — because if for any reason this doesn't work the way I'm describing, I want you to send it back and I will refund you completely. No questions, no pressure, no catch. There is genuinely no financial risk in trying this. I designed it that way on purpose.
