I Couldn't Start My Own Leaf Blower Anymore. So I Built a Better One.

A personal letter from David Mercer, founder of Seese Pro™ — on getting older, staying capable, and the tool I wish I'd had ten years ago.

Dear Friend,

Dear friend,

I want to tell you something I've never admitted publicly. Two autumns ago, I stood in my driveway for a full fifteen minutes — not because I didn't know what to do, but because I was dreading it. In front of me sat my gas leaf blower: a machine I'd owned for eleven years, a machine I once used without a second thought. That morning, all I could think about was the pull cord that never caught on the first try, the dead weight of the thing pressing into my right shoulder, the vibration that traveled straight into my joints and settled there, and the near-certain knowledge that by the next morning I'd be stiff and sore in ways that used to take a hard week of physical labor to earn. I was 58 years old. I was still healthy, still active, still perfectly capable of taking care of my own home. And yet the tools I'd trusted my whole life had quietly, without any ceremony, started working against me instead of with me. I didn't tell my wife. I didn't tell my doctor. I just stood there in the driveway that morning, and I made a decision that changed the next two years of my life.

When the Garage Became a Graveyard of Good Intentions

My name is David Mercer. I spent twenty-two years as a product engineer designing power tools — the kind contractors and landscapers depend on every single day. I understood motors, torque curves, airflow dynamics, fatigue load on handles. I understood, or thought I understood, what made a tool genuinely good. What I failed to account for — what nobody ever talks about in the product specification sheets — is that a tool's real value isn't measured in watts or RPMs or cubic feet per minute. It's measured in whether you actually reach for it. My gas blower had specs I was genuinely proud of. Eight pounds of solid machinery, capable of moving wet leaf piles in a single pass. For years, I used it without thinking. Then my right shoulder started giving me trouble after a ski accident. My lower back started sending its own quiet complaints — the kind that don't stop you, but that color everything you do for the next forty-eight hours. And the machine I'd been proud of slowly transformed, in my mind, from a tool into a test. I began postponing the yard work the way you postpone an uncomfortable conversation — not today, maybe this weekend, it's not that bad yet. Until it was. Until I watched my neighbor Carol — a retired schoolteacher somewhere in her early seventies — spend forty-five minutes sweeping her front walkway entirely by hand, because she told me her old blower was just too much trouble to drag out anymore. I looked at Carol. I looked at my garage. And I thought something I'd never let myself think before: this is a solved problem. It just hasn't been solved for people like us.

What I Actually Set Out to Build

I didn't start a company. I started with a single honest question: why does clearing my driveway have to cost me two days of back pain? That was the entire brief I gave myself. Not build a better blower — just make this one ordinary task stop being a physical event I had to budget recovery time around. I started buying every cordless blower I could find. Twelve different models, ranging from forty dollars to over two hundred, spread across my workbench like an investigation. And I found the same fundamental compromise baked into every single one. The lighter models were gentle on your joints but couldn't move serious debris. The powerful ones had real airflow, but they weighed almost as much as the gas machines I was trying to get away from. Nobody had cracked both problems at once — and after tearing them apart, I knew exactly why. They were all built around brushed motor technology that's been in handheld tools since the early 1980s. Reliable, cheap, widely understood — and fundamentally limited in power-to-weight ratio. Meanwhile, the aerospace and electric vehicle industries had moved on entirely. Brushless motors — the same class of motor you'll find inside high-performance drones and Tesla drivetrains — deliver dramatically more power per gram, run cooler, last far longer because there are no friction parts to wear down, and can be made remarkably compact. There was no engineering reason you couldn't put one inside a handheld residential blower. It just hadn't been done yet at a price that made sense for someone maintaining their own home. So I did it. Fourteen months of prototypes. Six versions that went straight in the bin. Hundreds of hours of testing across a full calendar year in my own backyard — fall leaves, winter snow on the front steps, a spring full of pollen and debris, a summer washing my car and trying to dry it without dragging out a chamois cloth. The result was a unit that produces 200 miles per hour of continuous airflow from a motor spinning at 30,000 RPM — housed in a body weighing exactly 950 grams. That's slightly less than a bag of sugar. Light enough to operate comfortably with one hand. Powerful enough to strip wet, compacted leaves off a brick patio in a single pass.

I'll also tell you this: the day I realized I'd built something genuinely useful wasn't the day I checked the airspeed numbers. It was the evening I handed the prototype to my wife Sandra — who has not, in thirty-one years of marriage, ever voluntarily touched a power tool — and she held it in one hand, blew the leaves off our front steps, set it down, and said: "That's actually kind of fun." That was the moment I knew.

The Emails I Wasn't Expecting

I launched Seese Pro quietly, about eighteen months ago. I had imagined our early customers would be younger — people who'd never owned a gas blower, apartment owners with small balconies, that kind of profile. What actually happened was something I didn't see coming at all. Within the first few weeks, the emails started arriving. And almost all of them were from people in their sixties and seventies. Retired teachers. Former nurses. Widows living alone in houses they'd owned for thirty years, determined to keep caring for them. Men who'd spent their whole working lives doing their own maintenance and were not, under any circumstances, prepared to stop. People who weren't shopping for a gadget. They were looking for a way to keep going on their own terms. Those emails changed how I thought about what I'd built.

"I've been letting my son come do the yard for the past two years because I just couldn't manage the equipment anymore. My back, my shoulder — it stopped being worth it. Last Saturday I did the whole thing myself for the first time since his last visit. I don't know how to explain to you what that felt like. I sat on the porch when I was done and felt like myself again."
— Robert T., 67, Portland OR

I read that email four times sitting at my desk. Because I understood it in a way that no marketing document could ever come close to capturing. It wasn't about leaves. It wasn't about the driveway. It was about the quiet, entirely private dignity of doing something yourself — of not having to call, not having to wait, not having to feel like a person who used to be able to do this. That's what I had been reaching for when I built this thing. I just didn't realize, until those emails started coming in, how many other people needed exactly the same thing. I had built a tool for myself. I had accidentally built it for a generation.

"I got rid of my gas blower after twenty years. I was honestly embarrassed to admit I just couldn't handle it anymore. The Seese Pro fits in my hand, I can run it for forty minutes without stopping, and my driveway looks better than it has in years. My neighbor asked what I was using. She ordered one the same afternoon."
— Margaret H., 71, Columbus OH
