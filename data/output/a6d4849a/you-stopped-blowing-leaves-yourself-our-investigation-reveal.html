You stopped blowing leaves yourself. Not because you didn't want to. Because your back didn't give you a choice.

That's what Barbara, 67, from Phoenix told us — almost word for word — when we found her post on a neighborhood forum. The caption under her photo of a freshly swept patio read: "First time in two years I did this myself. I actually cried a little."

We called her. Then we called 47 other homeowners between 55 and 74 who had posted something similar. What we found was the same story, told in a hundred different ways — and at the center of every single one of them, a device that weighs less than a liter of milk.

What Our Investigation Found

Our editorial team spent three weeks investigating the growing segment of ultra-light cordless blowers — and what we uncovered about the traditional outdoor power equipment industry is not flattering.

  • Finding #1: The average residential gas leaf blower weighs between 18 and 25 pounds. To operate one, you need two hands, a healthy lumbar spine, and enough grip strength to yank a pull-start cord repeatedly until the engine catches. For millions of homeowners over 55, that combination is no longer a given.
  • Finding #2: Outdoor power equipment injuries — herniated discs, rotator cuff tears, falls caused by loss of balance under load — send tens of thousands of Americans to emergency rooms each year. Homeowners over 55 represent a disproportionate share of those hospital visits.
  • Finding #3: The majority of seniors who stop doing their own yard work do not do it by choice. They do it because the tools they grew up with have become physically incompatible with the body they have today.
  • Finding #4: A new category of ultra-light, brushless cordless blowers weighing under one kilogram has been gaining ground for two years. They do not look like the tools people expect. But they outperform them in the ways that matter most to the people who actually use them.
  • Finding #5: The Seese Pro Ultra-Light Blower, at exactly 950 grams, has emerged as the most-discussed device in this category. We ordered one. We tested it for 30 days. Here is what we found.

"The Heaviest Thing About These Tools Is the Price Your Body Pays"

We contacted Dr. Karen Simms, a certified occupational therapist and ergonomics consultant who has worked with aging adults and senior workers for over 22 years. We described the tools most homeowners over 60 are still using — gas blowers between 18 and 25 pounds, push brooms requiring sustained forward pressure, chamois cloths that demand awkward bending and arm extension — and asked for her clinical perspective.

"The problem with traditional outdoor power tools is not the task itself," she told us. "It is the weight, the vibration, and the grip demand sustained over time. When you ask a 65-year-old body to repeatedly handle something that weighs 20 pounds — with resistance, vibration, and a pull-start mechanism — you are not just risking fatigue. You are risking a disc, a rotator cuff, a loss of balance. And the worst part is that most people push through it anyway, because asking for help feels worse than the pain."

We described the Seese Pro to her: 950 grams, one-hand operation, push-button start, no gas, no vibration beyond the airflow itself.

"That," she said, "is what this category of tools should have looked like fifteen years ago. The technology existed. The will to design for aging users apparently did not."

What the Power Equipment Industry Would Rather You Not Calculate

Here is the part of this story that gave our editorial team pause.

The brushless turbine motor technology at the core of the Seese Pro has been commercially available for years. The engineering required to pack it into a sub-kilogram housing has been feasible for at least a decade. The demand from an aging homeowner population has existed for longer than that.

So why has no major outdoor power brand produced something like this for the mainstream consumer market?

  • Gas equipment is a recurring revenue ecosystem. The initial purchase is only the beginning. Fuel, oil, spark plug replacements, carburetor cleanings, seasonal tune-ups — a gas-powered blower generates years of downstream spend. A sealed brushless unit that requires zero maintenance and carries a lifetime replacement warranty does not.
  • The industry built a mythology around weight. Decades of marketing have trained consumers to associate heaviness with power. A contractor-grade tool weighing 25 pounds reads as serious. A 950-gram device reads as a toy — until you hold one and realize the airflow is coming out at 200 miles per hour.
  • Seniors were not the design target. The baseline user imagined by most outdoor power tool designers is a physically fit adult in their 30s or 40s. The person actually buying a leaf blower on a Saturday morning in suburban America is increasingly in their 60s — and the tools have not moved to meet them.

The Seese Pro was built for exactly that gap. One hand. No back engagement. No gas, no maintenance, no startup ritual. No reason left to avoid the yard work you used to do without thinking twice.
