Last October, Albert Renaud Yanked His Starter Cord. The Crack He Heard Wasn't the Engine. It Was His L4-L5 Disc.

By Editorial Team | March 30, 2026 | 8:47 am

HERO IMAGE

Albert Renaud, 68, from Dayton, Ohio, holding his back in pain while standing in his garage next to a heavy gas leaf blower

Albert Renaud had been managing his two-acre property in Dayton, Ohio for thirty-seven years. Every autumn, without fail, he'd walk to his garage, grab the starter cord of his 24-pound Stihl gas blower, and pull. It was routine. Ordinary. The kind of thing millions of American homeowners do every single fall weekend.

Last October, something changed.

The cord was stiff that morning. Albert was already frustrated—his back had been bothering him for months, a dull ache that came and went. He yanked harder than usual. The crack that echoed through the garage wasn't the engine turning over. It was his spine.

The next six months were a blur: an emergency MRI revealed a herniated disc at L4-L5. Surgery. Three months of physical therapy. A $14,200 medical bill his insurance didn't fully cover. He couldn't mow. Couldn't rake. Couldn't even pick up his grandchildren without sharp pain shooting down his left leg.

"I thought I'd have to hire someone to maintain my yard for the rest of my life," Albert told us from his kitchen table, still moving carefully. "At sixty-eight, I figured that was it. This was my new normal."

The Injury Nobody Warns You About

INFOGRAPHIC

Visual breakdown showing: Gas blower weight progression, L4-L5 disc herniation diagram, 47,000 annual US back injuries from yard equipment, and recovery timeline chart

Albert's story isn't unique. In fact, it's shockingly common.

The Consumer Product Safety Commission reports that approximately 47,000 Americans suffer back and spinal injuries from yard equipment every single year. The vast majority? Homeowners over fifty-five. People like Albert. People like maybe you—or someone in your family right now.

Here's what the manufacturers don't advertise: traditional gas leaf blowers weigh between 22 and 28 pounds. That's almost two bags of flour. When you're fifty-five, sixty, seventy years old, your core strength isn't what it was at thirty. Your discs have lost water content. Your spinal ligaments are less flexible. A sudden jerk—like pulling a stubborn starter cord—can herniate a disc instantly. No warning. No second chance.

Margaret Chen, a retired nurse practitioner from Portland, Oregon, experienced something similar. "My dad was seventy-two when he injured his back with a gas blower," she shared. "He's an independent man. That one injury cost him eight thousand dollars in medical bills and his ability to maintain his property. It broke something in him emotionally, not just physically."

The Cord-Pull Risk: A single forceful yank on a starter cord creates sudden, violent torque through your lower spine. For anyone over fifty-five with any history of back pain, this single motion can cause permanent injury.

The problem runs deeper than injuries, though. Gas blowers are expensive to maintain. Oil changes. Spark plugs. Seasonal tune-ups. They're loud—so loud that neighbors complain and your hearing suffers. They emit blue smoke and fumes. And they require genuine strength and balance to operate safely, especially on uneven terrain or when you're tired.

Many homeowners over sixty simply give up. They hire yard services that cost $1,200 to $2,400 per year. They watch their independence slip away. "I couldn't imagine not being able to maintain my own property," Albert said, his voice tight with emotion. "It felt like I was aging overnight."

Why the Problem Has Never Been Solved Until Now

Here's what's infuriating: the technology to solve this problem has existed for years. Lithium-ion batteries. Brushless motors. Aerodynamic turbine design. But the major manufacturers—Stihl, Husqvarna, Echo—have zero incentive to build a truly lightweight, safe alternative. Why? Because their gas blower business generates billions in annual revenue. From the equipment itself to the oil, plugs, repairs, and dealer networks, they've built an entire ecosystem around products that are heavy, high-maintenance, and dangerous.

The cordless blowers that do exist are either underpowered toys that can barely move wet leaves, or they're still absurdly heavy—sixteen, eighteen, twenty pounds—which defeats the entire purpose.

Nobody was solving the real problem: creating a blower lightweight enough for seniors, powerful enough for real work, and safe enough to use without risking a herniated disc.

Nobody, that is, until Albert decided to build one himself.

What Albert Built in His Garage

Albert spent eighteen months in his garage. He studied turbine aerodynamics. He tested seventeen different battery configurations. He worked with a mechanical engineer from Cleveland State University. He interviewed orthopedic surgeons about spinal safety. His goal was obsessive: create a leaf blower so light, so powerful, and so easy to use that someone with a bad back could safely maintain a two-acre property without pain.

The breakthrough came when Albert stopped trying to match gas blower power and instead reimagined what "enough power" actually means. A gas blower at 950 RPM sounds impressive. But studies showed that for residential lawn work—clearing leaves, debris, and light grass clippings—you only need sustained airspeed of around 180 to 200 MPH. Anything beyond that is wasted fuel and wasted weight.

So Albert built to that spec. A brushless motor spinning at 30,000 RPM. A turbine design that generates 200+ MPH airspeed. And a weight target: under 2.2 pounds.

The result: the Seese Pro V9. A cordless leaf blower that weighs just 2.1 pounds—less than a can of paint—delivers 200+ MPH of airflow, runs for forty minutes on a single charge, and starts with a single button press. No yanking. No straining. No risk.
