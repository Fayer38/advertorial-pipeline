The Morning I Almost Called My Son

It was a Sunday morning in late October. The kind of morning you love when you're in your sixties — cool air, golden light, the smell of fallen leaves drifting across the yard you've tended for thirty years. I stood on the back porch, coffee in hand, staring at the flagstone patio buried under a carpet of oak leaves. Then I looked at the gas blower sitting in the corner of the garage, and I felt something I hadn't expected to feel in my own home: dread.

Not fear. Not laziness. Just the specific dread that comes when you know exactly what something is going to cost you. My old blower weighed close to twelve pounds fully loaded. And I'd learned, the hard way, that twenty minutes of fighting it meant Monday morning would arrive with a lower back that had its own opinions about the matter.

I stood there a long moment. Then I took out my phone and nearly typed my son's name. I stopped. Set the phone down on the porch rail. Put on my jacket. Went to get the blower. Because I wasn't ready to be the person who calls his son to deal with a pile of leaves.

The Tax I Didn't Know I Was Paying

The honest truth is, I hadn't stopped doing yard work. I'd just been doing it badly — and paying for it afterward, every single time. I'm 63 years old. I built a life in this house. Poured thirty years into every corner of this property. And somewhere around my late fifties, the tools I'd always relied on started to feel like they were working against me. Not all at once. Just slowly, incrementally, season by season.

The rake felt heavier than it used to. The blower more unwieldy. The simple satisfaction of a clean patio started coming with a price tag attached. I'd push through a Sunday afternoon feeling strong, even a little proud of myself. Then Monday would arrive. The kind of Monday where rolling out of bed required a few seconds of planning, where a low, steady ache in my lower back was just part of the morning routine. I told myself it was normal. That this was just what getting older looked like.

What nobody talks about are the small humiliations that come along with it. The way you lean on the fence post to catch your breath and glance sideways hoping the neighbors aren't watching. The way your daughter says, "Dad, just let me hire someone" — with that particular mixture of love and concern that somehow makes everything feel worse. The way you nod and say, "Maybe." But you don't mean it. You're not ready to mean it. And that gap between what you can no longer do without pain and what you flat-out refuse to give up — that gap is where a lot of quiet frustration lives.

"Every Sunday I did the yard. Every Monday I paid for it. I had accepted that as just the way things were now. I was wrong about that."

The Neighbor, the Back Fence, and the Thing That Looked Too Small to Work

It was my neighbor Bill who first mentioned it — almost in passing, the way neighbors do when something is on their mind but they don't want to oversell it. We were standing on opposite sides of the back fence one afternoon and he said, "Hey, you seen those lightweight cordless blowers? My wife got one. Thing's absurd." I remember exactly what I thought: great, another gadget. I'd been down that road. Battery-powered tools that ran out of charge halfway through the job. Supposedly lightweight models that were lighter than my old gas blower but still nothing a tired back wanted to fight with for thirty minutes in the cold.

But then Bill held it up over the fence, and I stopped being skeptical.

It looked wrong, somehow. Too compact. Like a power tool that hadn't finished growing. "That's it?" I asked. He smiled. "200 miles an hour of air out of that little thing. I did the whole driveway in four minutes." I went inside and looked it up. The Seese Pro Ultra-Light Blower. 950 grams — lighter than a bag of flour. One-hand operation. A brushless motor, the same technology used in high-performance electric vehicles and competition drones. No gas, no cord, no choke cable, no pull-start. One button. I spent about twenty minutes reading everything I could find about it. Then I ordered one.

The First Time I Used It, I Actually Laughed Out Loud

It arrived four days later in a box about the size of a shoebox. I remember picking it up off the front porch and laughing — that involuntary laugh you make when something confirms your expectations in a way you didn't quite believe it would. It weighed almost nothing. Or rather, it weighed exactly what they'd advertised: just under a kilogram. I held it in one hand, turned it over a few times, looked at it. Charged it overnight. Waited for morning.

The next day was a Thursday — gray sky, wet leaves plastered to the flagstone, the kind of morning where the old version of me would have put it off until the weekend. I took the Seese Pro outside to the back patio. Here is what I remember most clearly: the sound of it spinning up. This high, clean whir — nothing like the coughing, smoky roar of my old gas blower. And then the air hit the leaves, and they moved. Fast. Decisively. I swept the entire back patio — forty square meters of flagstone — in just under five minutes. One hand. Standing fully upright. Not bracing myself against the weight of the machine, not fighting to keep it aimed in the right direction, not stopping to rest. Just doing it. The way yard work is supposed to feel. Like it was easy. Because it genuinely was.

That weekend I timed myself drying the car after a wash. Three minutes, roof to bumper, no microfiber towels, no scratching, no leaning awkwardly across the hood with both arms. And when the first real frost hit in November, I walked out to the driveway, held up the Seese Pro with one hand, and cleared the windshield in ninety seconds. No scraper. No frozen fingers working at a sheet of ice. Just clean, controlled air doing the job cleanly.

In my first full month with the Seese Pro, I used it on the patio, the driveway, the garage floor, both cars, and the shelving in the garage. The battery gives forty minutes per charge, and I never once ran out mid-job. I never once put it down with a sore arm. That had not happened with any single tool in my garage for the better part of a decade.

My wife walked out while I was working on the front walk and stopped right in her tracks. "Is that all you're using?" I grinned the way I haven't grinned doing yard work in years. "Yep." That was the moment I knew I'd made the right call.

My Daughter Stopped Suggesting I Hire Someone

I want to tell you about a Sunday morning in November, about three weeks after I got the Seese Pro. Same back porch. Same coffee. Same golden light. But this time there was no dread when I looked at the yard. The leaves were deep that morning — the kind of coverage that would have taken me close to an hour with the old setup, including breaks, including that low familiar ache settling into my lumbar by minute fifteen. I picked up the Seese Pro, went out, and came back inside thirty-five minutes later. Property clean. Second cup of coffee. Back feeling exactly the way it should feel on a Sunday morning.

That is what I keep returning to: how much of my weekend energy I had been quietly spending on recovering from the weekend itself. I'd been doing it for years without fully recognizing it for what it was. The fatigue I dragged into Sunday evenings wasn't just normal tiredness from a day well spent — it was the tax my tools had been levying on my body, every single week, season after season. The Seese Pro doesn't collect that tax. I do the same work. I feel better afterward. That sounds like a small thing. It isn't a small thing. It turns out to be almost everything.

I still do everything myself. The yard, the car, the driveway, the porch, the garage. My daughter stopped suggesting I hire someone — not because I said anything to her about it, but because she can see with her own eyes that it isn't a problem anymore. She sees me outside on a Sunday afternoon moving at my own pace, standing straight, not wincing, not leaning on anything. She said to me recently, "You seem happier out there, Dad."

That is probably the most accurate description of what this one tool actually changed. Not just the time I save. Not just the back pain I'm no longer having. Just that I'm happier out there — doing the same work I've always done, on my own terms, by myself. The way I've always wanted to do it. Still.
